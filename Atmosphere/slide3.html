Pascal, Atmospheres, Bar, Millibar & Hectopascal

Reference: All units on this page describe the same physical quantity — atmospheric pressure. The standard reference is 1 Atmosphere (Atm), defined as the average air pressure at sea level.
1 Atm = 101,325 Pa, where 1 Pa = 1 N / 1 m²
Pascal
Pa
1 Atm = 101,325 Pa
The metric (SI) base unit of pressure. Named after French physicist and mathematician Blaise Pascal (1623–1662). Defined as one Newton of force per one square meter of area. The Pascal is a very small unit — standard atmospheric pressure is over 100,000 Pa, so larger derived units are often more convenient.
Atmosphere
Atm
1 Atm = 101,325 Pa (exact)
A non-SI but widely used unit defined as the average atmospheric pressure at sea level. Convenient for expressing any pressure relative to Earth's atmospheric pressure. At sea level, for 1 Atm, water boils at 100°C and freezes at 0°C.
Bar
bar
1 Atm = 1.01325 bar
A metric unit of pressure introduced in 1909. Exactly 100,000 Pa (or 100 kPa). Because 1 bar is very close to 1 Atm, it is a convenient unit to use for many scientific and industrial applications — particularly in oceanography, meteorology, and thermodynamics. Standard atmosphere is just 1.3% greater than 1 bar.
Millibar
mbar or mb
1 Atm = 1,013.25 mbar
One thousandth of a bar (1 mbar = 0.001 bar = 100 Pa). The millibar was the standard unit in meteorology for most of the 20th century, and is still widely used in weather maps, aviation weather reports, and weather discussions. Atmospheric pressure near the eye of a powerful hurricane can drop to 870–900 mbar.
Hectopascal
hPa
1 Atm = 1,013.25 hPa  |  1 hPa ≡ 1 mbar (numerically identical)
One hundred Pascals (hecto = 100, so 1 hPa = 100 Pa). The hectopascal was adopted by the World Meteorological Organization (WMO) as the preferred unit for surface pressure. It is numerically identical to the millibar, which made the transition seamless: a surface pressure of 1013.25 mbar is exactly 1013.25 hPa. Modern weather reports, ship logs, and aviation settings all use hPa.

Blaise Pascal (1623–1662)

French physicist, mathematician, and inventor, Blaise Pascal made foundational contributions to fluid mechanics and the study of pressure. He demonstrated that atmospheric pressure decreases with altitude — an experiment carried out on Puy de Dôme in 1648 — and invented one of the earliest mechanical calculators.

His work on hydrostatics established the principle that now bears his name. In his honor, the SI unit of pressure — the Pascal (Pa) — was named, defined as one Newton of force per square meter of area.

Pascal's Principle

An external pressure applied to a liquid is extended, undiminished, to every point in the liquid, and to the walls of the liquid's container.
