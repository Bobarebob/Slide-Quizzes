Scientific Methodology: Hydrometry via Buoyancy

1. Density & Alcohol Proof

Ethanol has a specific gravity of 0.789, whereas distilled water has a specific gravity of 1.000. When mixed, they create a liquid with a density proportional to their volumetric ratio. By definition, 50 Proof Whiskey contains exactly 25% ABV (Alcohol By Volume).

Because sugar syrup is extremely dense (~1.300), sweet cocktails have high specific gravity, making ice float high even if they contain high ABV. This is called "density masking" in hydrometry!

2. The Physics of Floating

According to Archimedes' Principle, a floating body displaces a volume of fluid whose weight is equal to the weight of the body. Since the weight of the ice cube is $W = \text{SG}_{\text{ice}} \cdot V_{\text{total}} \cdot \rho_{\text{water}} \cdot g$ and the buoyant force is $F_b = \text{SG}_{\text{liquid}} \cdot V_{\text{submerged}} \cdot \rho_{\text{water}} \cdot g$, equating them gives:

$$\text{SG}_{\text{liquid}} = \frac{\text{SG}_{\text{ice}}}{f}$$

Where $f = V_{\text{submerged}} / V_{\text{total}}$ is the fraction of the ice cube under the liquid surface.

3. The Physics of Sinking

If the liquid's specific gravity is less than the ice cube's specific gravity ($\text{SG}_{\text{liquid}} < \text{SG}_{\text{ice}} = 0.917$), the upward buoyant force is insufficient to balance gravity. The ice cube sinks. The downward net force is: $$F_{\text{net}} = m_{\text{ice}} \cdot g - F_b = m_{\text{ice}} \cdot g_{\text{apparent}}$$

Substituting masses and volumes yields the apparent gravity $g_{\text{apparent}}$:

$$g_{\text{apparent}} = g \cdot \left(1 - \frac{\text{SG}_{\text{liquid}}}{\text{SG}_{\text{ice}}}\right)$$

By tracking this downward acceleration rate, the machine computes the exact specific gravity: $$\text{SG}_{\text{liquid}} = \text{SG}_{\text{ice}} \cdot \left(1 - \frac{g_{\text{apparent}}}{g}\right)$$
