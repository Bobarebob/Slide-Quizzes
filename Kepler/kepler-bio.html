Johannes Kepler

The Mathematician Who Unlocked the Heavens

🎧 Instructions

On this page we learn about Johannes Kepler, a first-class mathematician, astronomer and astrologer, who derived by hand - from observational data collected by his colleague Tycho Brahe - Three Laws of Planetary Motion.

These three laws were ultimately derived by Sir Isaac Newton using his more fundamental Three Laws of Motion and his Law of Gravity, thus experimentally validating Newton's theoretical work.

Notably, Kepler's First Law showed that orbits of Solar System planets were ellipses (squished circles, or ovals), not pure circles. For this discovery, he primarily used Brahe's data on Mars.

Ultimately, Kepler's Third Law was used (posthumously) by Newton's friend, the astronomer Edmund Halley (of Halley's comet fame), to determine the average Sun-planet distances of all the Solar System planets, first by exploiting the relatively rare Transit of Venus to trigonometrically determine the value of the Astronomical Unit (AU), the average Sun-Earth distance (now known to be 93 million miles), and then second, plugging that number into Kepler's Third Law to determine the average Sun-planet distances of all the other planets known and tracked at that time (six planets total).

The Architect of Planetary Motion

Johannes Kepler (December 27, 1571 – November 15, 1630) was a German astronomer, mathematician, and astrologer who is best known for his revolutionary laws of planetary motion. His work fundamentally transformed our understanding of the solar system and laid the groundwork for Isaac Newton's theory of universal gravitation.

Born in the Free Imperial City of Weil der Stadt, Kepler studied theology and philosophy at the University of Tübingen, where he became a devoted follower of the heliocentric Copernican system. Despite facing numerous personal hardships, including religious persecution, poverty, and the loss of family members, Kepler persevered in his astronomical research.

Working with the meticulous observational data collected by Tycho Brahe, Kepler discovered that planets move in elliptical orbits rather than perfect circles, shattering centuries of astronomical tradition. His three laws of planetary motion, published between 1609 and 1619, described the motion of planets around the Sun with unprecedented accuracy.

Beyond his laws of planetary motion, Kepler made significant contributions to optics, discovered two new regular polyhedra, and provided a theoretical basis for logarithms. His work represented a crucial bridge between ancient and modern astronomy, combining rigorous mathematical analysis with empirical observation.
