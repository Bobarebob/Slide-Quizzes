Kepler's Second Law

The Law of Equal Areas

🎧 Instructions

On this page we see Kepler’s Second Law of Planetary Motion in action.

Like the First Law animation, we see four blue-colored animation control buttons: Pause/Resume, Reset, Slow Down, and Full Screen. Try all these buttons to see what they do. Note that the Slow Down and Full Screen buttons are toggle buttons, meaning press once for an action, and press again to reverse that action.

Additionally, we see on the top a planet/dwarf planet (Solar System object) selection bar. When an object is selected, it will start sweeping out equal areas in equal time intervals, just like what Mr. Kepler said.

In the upper left hand corner, the selected object’s eccentricity is displayed. Try selecting all of the objects! Which planet/dwarf planet has the lowest eccentricity, and which has the highest?

Also, for the object selected, its speed as it moves through its orbit is displayed, in both miles per second and kilometers per second. When the object attains its lowest speed, that speed is briefly displayed in red. When the object attains its highest speed, that speed is briefly displayed in green.

At what point in an object’s orbit is it going slowest? At what point in an object’s orbit is it going fastest?

Finally, choose Pluto specifically. Do the area chunks swept out have the same shape? If not, do the area chunks swept out have the same areas?

A line connecting a planet to the Sun sweeps out equal areas in equal time intervals.

This means planets move faster when closer to the Sun (at perihelion) and slower when farther away (at aphelion). The areas swept out are always equal for equal time periods, demonstrating conservation of angular momentum.

💡 Notice how the planet moves along an elliptical path, with the Sun (yellow) at one of the focal points. Choose each regular and dwarf planet to see how a higher planet eccentricity requires that the closer a planet gets to the Sun, the faster it must move in its orbit to "sweep out equal areas in equal times."
