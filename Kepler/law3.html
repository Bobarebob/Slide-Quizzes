Kepler's Third Law

The Law of Harmonies

🎧 Instructions

On this page we see Kepler’s Third Law of Planetary Motion in action.

Like the First and Second Law animations, we see four blue-colored animation control buttons: Pause/Resume, Reset, Slow Down, and Full Screen. Try all these buttons to see what they do. Note that the Slow Down and Full Screen buttons are toggle buttons, meaning press once for an action, and press again to reverse that action.

Additionally, we see on the top a planet selection bar. You can select up to three planets at a time. If you select an additional planet, it will remove one of the planets previously selected, according to a “first in first out” rule. Try any combination of planets you like!

For each planet selected, there will be on the upper left a color coded display of the planet’s semimajor axis (called out as the letter a, valued in astronomical units or AU), its period or time of journey around the Sun (called out as the letter T, valued in Earth years), and the ratio of T squared divided by a cubed (which according to Mr Kepler should have an approximate value of 1, in the appropriate units). Can you verify that - for any planet selected - this relationship is true?

What Edmund Halley realized is that once the value of the AU was determined (using the Transit of Venus), the actual distance to any of the other planets (which is roughly the value of a) can be easily calculated.

While Kepler’s Third Law doesn’t specifically state it, it can easily be shown that the farther away a planet is from the Sun, the slower it moves. Can you verify that from this animation?

The square of a planet's orbital period is proportional to the cube of its semi-major axis.

Mathematically: T² ∝ a³, where T is the orbital period in Earth years and a is the semi-major axis in astronomical units (AU). This means planets farther from the Sun take much longer to complete an orbit. When using these units, T²/a³ ≈ 1 for all planets.
