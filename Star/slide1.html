Origin of the First Elements

Primordial Nucleosynthesis & and the Three Epochs of Stars

🔊 Instructions

In the first twenty minutes after the Big Bang (roughly 13.8 billion years ago), the Universe was unimaginably hot and dense. Protons and neutrons were created (and then in some cases fused together) to form the first atomic nuclei — a process called Primordial Nucleosynthesis.

Primordial Nucleosynthesis
The formation of atomic nuclei during the first ~20 minutes after the Big Bang (sometimes called Big Bang Nucelosynthesis or BBN). The products were approximately 75% hydrogen and 25% helium by mass, with trace amounts of deuterium and tritium (hydrogen isotopes), and a smattering of the "metal" lithium.

Crucially, no heavier elements — carbon, oxygen, iron, gold — were produced during BBN. The Universe essentially began as a vast sea of hydrogen and helium, which eventually condensed into the first stars — and from that point on, stars themselves would forge every heavier element. As we'll see, astronomers divide stars into three populations — Population III, II, and I — based on when they formed and what raw material they formed from.

With some exotic exceptions, all elements heavier than helium were forged either inside stellar furnaces (C, O, Ne … Fe) or in the extreme energy of stellar explosions — supernovae — (Co, Ni … U) or neutron star collisions.

🌟 The Three Epochs or Populations of Stars — Populations III, II, and I

Astronomers classify stars into three populations based on when they formed and how rich they are in elements heavier than helium (what astronomers call "metals"). Each generation was born from the debris of the one before it.

Population III Stars — The First Stars

The very first stars formed roughly 200 million years after the Big Bang from pristine hydrogen and helium gas (and a touch of lithium) — as absolutely no heavier elements existed yet. Computer models suggest these stars were extraordinarily massive (hundreds of solar masses) and therefore extremely short-lived.

They died spectacularly in supernovae, seeding the Universe with the first sprinkling of carbon, oxygen, and heavier elements. No Population III stars survive today, although the James Webb Space Telescope (JWST) - looking far back in time - may have observed clumps of them.

Population II Stars — The Ancients

Formed starting ~200–400 million years after the Big Bang, Population II stars condensed from gas clouds that contained only hydrogen, helium, and small amounts of heavier elements ("metals") scattered by exploding Population III stars. Not much in the way of cosmic dust, these stars were considered "metal poor" compared to later Population I stars.

Because blue and yellow Population II stars burned their fuel quickly, most are gone. The only Population II stars we still observe locally today are Red Dwarfs, whose extraordinarily long lifespans (80–100 billion years for larger Red Dwarfs) far exceed the current age of the Universe.

Population I Stars — Children of Supernovae

When massive Population III and Population II stars exploded as supernovae, they scattered carbon, oxygen, silicon, iron, and dozens of other heavy elements ("metals") into space as cosmic dust. About 10 billion years ago (roughly 3.8 billion years after the Big Bang), new star-forming clouds could incorporate this dust — creating Population I stars rich in these heavy elements.

Our Sun is a Population I star, roughly 4.6 billion years old. All Solar System objects orbiting the Sun — including Earth — are literally made of recycled stellar ash: the stardust of long-dead stars.

Star furnaces are responsible for producing all elements from helium through iron via nuclear fusion in their cores. Elements heavier than iron — from cobalt to uranium — are created only during the extreme conditions of a supernova explosion, or possibly when two neutron stars collide. We are, quite literally, made of star stuff.
