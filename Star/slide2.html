🌌 How Many Stars Are There?

Astronomers in the early 20th century wrestled with a seemingly unanswerable question: how big is the Universe, and how many stars does it contain?

Henrietta Swan Leavitt (1868–1921), working at the Harvard Observatory, discovered a crucial tool. She noticed that Cepheid variable stars — pulsating giant stars — have a precise relationship between their period of pulsation and their intrinsic brightness. By measuring how long a Cepheid takes to brighten, dim, and brighten again, you can calculate exactly how luminous it truly is — and therefore how far away it must be.

Edwin Hubble (1889-1953), used Leavitt's period–luminosity law to measure the distance to "nebulae" — fuzzy patches of light — and proved they were entire galaxies far outside our own Milky Way. The Universe, it turned out, was vastly larger than anyone had imagined.

📊 Classifying the Stars — Cannon & the H-R Diagram

Annie Jump Cannon (1863–1941) — The Classifier

Working as one of "Pickering's Computers" at Harvard Observatory, Annie Jump Cannon analyzed thousands of photographic plates of stellar spectra and devised the star classification system still used today, seven total classes — with stars ordered by surface temperature, from hottest (O) to coolest (M):

O — Blue
B — Blue-white
A — White
F — Yellow-white
G — Yellow (Sun)
K — Orange
M — Red

Mnemonic: "Oh Boy, Astronomy Final's Gonna Kill Me."

Cannon's scheme ordered stars by their hydrogen absorption spectra, which turned out to correlate closely with intrinsic brightness, surface temperature, and size.

Astronomers Ejnar Hertzsprung and Henry Norris Russell independently plotted stellar surface temperature against intrinsic brightness (luminosity), creating the celebrated Hertzsprung-Russell (H-R) Diagram.

The H-R Diagram
A scatter plot of stars, with surface temperature (or spectral class) on the horizontal axis and intrinsic brightness (luminosity) on the vertical axis. Most stars fall along the diagonal Main Sequence band, from hot-bright blue stars (O, upper left) to cool-dim red stars (M, lower right). Our Sun is a mid-point G2 star, with a surface temperature of ~5800 K.

The hotter the star, the faster it burns through its hydrogen — a blue O star lives only ~20 million years; our yellow Sun will last ~10 billion years; a "large" red M dwarf endures for 80–100 billion years.
